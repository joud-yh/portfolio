Overview

A quiet space for overwhelmed teens· نبذة

Mishkat is designed for Arabic-speaking gifted adolescents aged 12–18 — students living with perfectionism, academic pressure, school burnout, and the kind of emotional overwhelm that comes from feeling everything too loudly.

The premise

Gifted teens don't always need treatment — sometimes they need a pause. Mishkat is not a medical or psychiatric tool. It does not diagnose, treat, or replace therapy. Instead, it offers short, gentle, emotionally safe micro-interventions: a 90-second breath, a one-line check-in, a small note, a calm game.

The goal is small steps — moments where a user can notice what they feel, set it down for a minute, and walk away feeling lighter, not lectured.

  • Audience Gifted adolescents · 12–18
  • Language Arabic-first (RTL)
  • Platform Mobile · Flutter-ready
  • Status Research-informed design system
  • Type Self-regulation, not treatment
The shift

From “fix yourself” to “let’s soften this a step”

Most wellness apps inherit the language of productivity — streaks, scores, missed days. For an already anxious teen, that language adds pressure on top of pressure. Mishkat was built around a single tonal shift.

What we avoid

Clinical labels. Streak guilt. Performance scores. Loud notifications that imply the user is behind. Anything that treats the user like a patient who needs to be repaired.

“أصلح نفسك.”

What we offer

One clear next step. Short activities, easy to start and easy to leave. Language that normalizes feelings without diagnosing them. A space that says: you don't need to carry everything alone.

“تعال نخففها خطوة.”

Design system

An emotional color language

Mishkat uses five soft hues — each one tied to an emotion. Color is never used alone: every mood selector pairs the color with a label and an icon, so users with color-vision differences (or just overwhelmed eyes) always get a non-color signal too.

Core features

Short, low-pressure activities· الميزات

Every activity in Mishkat is short — usually 1 to 5 minutes — and shaped to be easy to start and easy to leave. Success means the user tried, paused, noticed, or took one small step.

🌬️

Breathing exercises

An expanding-shrinking circle that paces inhale, hold, exhale. The counter changes; nothing is faked.

1–3 min
🌿

Mindfulness

Grounding, affect labeling, and body awareness — one step per screen, no timer pressure.

2–4 min

Emotional mini-games

1–3 min
✍️

Expressive writing

3–5 min

Design principles

What Mishkat is — and what it never is

These rules sat above every screen, every line of copy, every animation. They were the filter for every design decision.

✓   Mishkat is

  • Calm and emotionally safe
  • Soft, minimal, visually breathable
  • Short interactions, easy to leave
  • Language that normalizes feelings
  • One clear next step in stressed states
  • Research-informed, evidence-aware

×   Mishkat is not

  • A medical or psychiatric treatment app
  • Aggressively gamified or competitive
  • Streak-based or guilt-driven
  • Clinical, corporate, or childish in tone
  • A therapy replacement
  • A place that punishes mistakes

Challenges

Three real design tensions· التحديات

Designing for emotional safety in Arabic, for teenagers, without sliding into childishness or clinical coldness — every layer had a tension to solve.

01

Calm without being childish

The risk: soft colors and rounded shapes can quickly read as “kids app.” But the audience is teens — they would dismiss anything that felt below their age.

Resolved through editorial minimalism — generous spacing, restrained illustration, mature typography, and abstract shapes in games instead of cartoon characters. Calm, not cute.

Tone
02

Arabic-first, without translation feel

Most wellness UX vocabulary is born in English. Direct translation produced lines that felt either too clinical or too dramatic in Arabic.

The fix was writing Arabic-first — phrases like “لاحظ”, “خذ خطوة صغيرة”, “ما فيه إجابة صح أو غلط” — then checking the English brief, not the other way around. RTL layout, mirrored components, and Arabic-tuned line heights all followed from there.

Language
03

Engagement without gamification

Every product manager instinct says: add streaks, points, leaderboards. For this audience, all three would have caused real harm — guilt on missed days, comparison anxiety, fake achievement loops.

Mishkat replaces them with a Journey view: gentle weekly stats, emotional balance over time, and completion states that frame any try as success. The user is never behind.

Outcome
