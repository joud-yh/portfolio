A smart grocery companion that builds the shopping list for a household, then walks the shopper straight to the right shelf — designed and led end-to-end.

Overview

Shopping that knows the list — and the aisle· نبذة

Big supermarket runs are quietly exhausting: forgotten items, generic lists, and minutes lost wandering the aisles. Salltna uses IoT and AI to suggest a list tuned to your household, then points to the exact section and shelf for every product you pick.

The premise

On first use the app learns the basics — household size, consumption patterns — and proposes a ready-to-edit shopping list. From there it shows where each chosen item lives inside a partner supermarket, so the trip becomes a route, not a search.

I led this project as team leader: shaping the audience and journey, designing the wireframes and UI, and running the usability sessions that reshaped the navigation.

The shift

From a scribbled list to a guided trip

The everyday pain isn't buying — it's planning what to buy and then finding it. Salltna reframes both.

The friction

Shoppers write generic lists, forget recurring items, and lose time hunting for products across unfamiliar store layouts. Existing apps store a list — but they don't think with you, and they stop at the front door.

"I had the list. I just couldn't find half of it in the store."

The promise

A list the app helps build from your real consumption — fully editable — paired with in-store guidance down to the shelf, plus a view of partner supermarkets near you.

"Salltna plans the list, then maps the route."

Objectives

Four things the app must do well

01

Smart list suggestions

On first use, collect household data — members, consumption patterns — to propose a tailored shopping list.

02

Editable lists

Every suggestion stays fully editable, so the list bends to the user's real needs, not the other way around.

03

In-store product location

For each chosen item, surface its exact place in the supermarket — the section and the shelf.

04

Partner supermarkets

Let users browse and choose from supermarkets that collaborate with the app, then shop with confidence.

Research & analysis

Letting users organise the app

We ran a closed card-sort with five participants from the target audience, asking them to place app functionalities into four predefined categories. The patterns guided the whole information architecture.

CONSISTENCY

Home was obvious

Participants agreed on what belongs in Home — clear, shared understanding of its purpose.

VARIATION

Menu was contested

The Menu interface caused friction — people disagreed on how to categorise its elements.

OUTCOME

Four clean zones

The sort resolved into Home, Menu, Store Map, and Account — the app's final top-level map.

Challenges

The hard parts· التحديات

01

Designing around IoT + AI

Personalising a list and locating products implies real IoT and AI plumbing. We designed the experience to feel effortless while keeping the underlying logic — data in, tailored list out — honest and clear.

02

Organising the content

Card-sorting exposed real confusion around the Menu. We restructured the IA into four clean zones and regrouped related items so users stopped getting lost.

03

Serving very different users

Families, tech-savvy individuals, and small offices want different things. We anchored decisions to the simplest user — a parent with limited tech experience — so the app stayed clear for everyone.

04

Leading a team

As leader I had to keep four designers aligned on one visual language and flow. Shared components and a single source of truth in Figma kept the screens coherent.

Usability testing

What four users told us

We tested with 4–5 participants aged 20–45, technical and non-technical, on a representative store map. Their feedback drove real changes.

The home page didn't really serve the app's main purpose for me.

User 1 · navigation

The layout and the menu icon were unclear — I struggled to move around.

User 2 · clarity

Group related items, like drinks and dairy, so I spend less effort finding them.

User 3 · list grouping

No issues at all — the interfaces were easy to use.

User 4 · smooth run
What we changed: reworked the home page around the core task, clarified the menu icon and layout, and regrouped list items by category so locating products takes fewer steps.
