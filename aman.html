لأن سلامة كل حاج أمانة

A safety-critical wearable + dashboard system for the Night of Nafrah — a passive smart bracelet that watches over each pilgrim and alerts the campaign leader the moment something goes wrong, even when the network is down.

🕋
Overview

A system, not just an app· نبذة

During Nafrah — the night movement from Arafat to Muzdalifah — pilgrims get separated, exhausted, and out of reach while the cellular network buckles under the crowd. Aman treats this as a safety-critical interaction system spanning a wearable, a leader dashboard, and a mesh network — designed around the pilgrims least able to ask for help.

The premise

Every pilgrim wears a passive smart bracelet that quietly reads heart rate, temperature, motion, and distance from the group. A four-state model — Normal, Warning, Danger, Emergency — decides when to nudge the pilgrim with light and vibration, and when to alert the campaign leader's dashboard.

When the mobile network fails, alerts still travel hop-by-hop over BLE Mesh. If the leader doesn't respond in time, the system escalates on its own to medical support.

The context

Why Nafrah is the hardest night

We deliberately chose the single most demanding moment of Hajj — the after-sunset movement to Muzdalifah — because it stacks every risk on top of the others.

CROWD

6–8 people / m²

Density at bottlenecks runs well past the safe limit of four, so calling out and eye contact stop working.

NETWORK

Cellular blackout

Over two million pilgrims move in a few hours; the mobile network saturates and phone-based help fails.

TIME

A religious deadline

Pilgrims must reach Muzdalifah before midnight for combined prayer — pressure on an already tiring walk.

FATIGUE

Hours of standing

After a full day at Arafat, heat retention and exhaustion build quietly — and can turn into a medical event.

The shift

From hoping someone notices, to knowing

Today a separated or struggling pilgrim depends on a leader's eyes and a working phone. Aman replaces both with continuous, network-independent sensing.

Without Aman

Eyes and phones

The leader can't watch 80 pilgrims at once. When the network drops and a tired pilgrim drifts off, no one knows until it's late — help can arrive an hour or more after collapse.

With Aman

A bracelet that speaks for him

The bracelet senses separation and deterioration early, asks for help on the pilgrim's behalf with light and vibration, and reaches the leader over mesh — even with zero signal.

Human state

Designed around Abu Salem· الشخصية

Our primary persona is the pilgrim most at risk and least able to use technology — if the system works for him, it works for everyone.

Mohammad Aslam Khan · "Abu Salem"

A 58-year-old retired textile worker from Faisalabad who saved for fifteen years to perform Hajj. He speaks only Urdu and Punjabi, can't read Arabic or English, and has never worn a tracker. He lives with type 2 diabetes and hypertension.

At 8:30 PM on Nafrah night: separated from his group, he hasn't eaten since 2 PM, has missed his evening medication, and his phone is at 8%. His heart rate hits 132, skin temperature 38.1°C, and blood sugar drops to 78 — yet cultural reserve keeps him from asking strangers for help.

Sensing strategy

One bracelet, six signals

A single wearable keeps deployment cheap and failure points few. No alert fires on one reading alone — signals are always combined to keep false alarms under control.

❤️

Heart rate

Optical PPG every 30s — physical strain, stress, possible low blood sugar.

🌡️

Skin temperature

Thermistor every 60s — late heat retention and heat-stress risk.

📍

Location

GNSS / GPS — distance from the group and last known position.

🤸

Motion

IMU accelerometer + gyro — falls, sudden stops, long stillness.

📡

Proximity

BLE signal — separation from the group, more reliable than GPS in crowds.

🆘

SOS button

A 3-second press — explicit help that overrides every other rule.

Interpretation logic

A four-state safety model

Every reading maps to one of four states. Transitions need two or three signals to agree within a time window — and the system can de-escalate back to Normal once the pilgrim recovers.

Green · silent

Normal

Everything in range. Calm green ring, no vibration; the dashboard shows a green dot.

Yellow · light buzz

Warning

Early drift or strain — e.g. 80m away for two minutes. A gentle nudge: "drink water".

Red · strong buzz

Danger

HR over 130 with stillness, or true separation. Red flash and an urgent alert to the leader.

Mesh broadcast

Emergency

A fall, critical vitals, or SOS. The bracelet broadcasts location and vitals to medical support.

Diabetic-aware rule: for Abu Salem, a raised heart rate plus stillness plus four hours since his last meal jumps straight to Danger — catching probable hypoglycemia before collapse.

Interaction design

Two surfaces, one safety system

The pilgrim never reads a screen; the leader never hunts for information. Each side is tuned to its user.

On the wrist

Three channels only — a coloured LED ring, three vibration patterns, and an SOS button. No text, no app, no setup. Green means safe, yellow means slow down, red asks bystanders for help. It falls silent on its own during the five daily prayers.

On the dashboard

A single calm screen for the leader: a live map of the group, status badges, and any urgent case pinned at the top with the pilgrim's vitals, medical context, and location — plus two actions: go to him, or call the Red Crescent.

Coordination

Who responds, and when

Aman is multi-actor by design. Each role gets exactly the signal it needs — and the chain keeps moving even if one link is unavailable.

🧕

Pilgrim

Wears the bracelet; receives light and haptic guidance, presses SOS if needed.

🧑‍✈️

Campaign leader

First responder — gets the alert, the location, and the medical context to act fast.

📶

Nearby bracelets

Act as mesh relays, forwarding alerts hop-by-hop when there's no network.

🚑

Red Crescent

Receives escalated emergencies with live vitals and location for medical response.

🛂

Authorities

Notified on mass events — clustered danger cases trigger a coordinated response.

No silent failures: if the leader doesn't acknowledge a danger alert within 30 seconds, the system bypasses him and escalates to medical support automatically.
Trust & resilience

The risks I designed against

A safety system is only as good as people's trust in it. I mapped the main risks and the mitigation built into the design for each.

HIGH

Privacy of health data

Edge processing on the bracelet, pseudonymous IDs, encryption, consent in the pilgrim's language, and auto-deletion.

HIGH

False alarms

Alerts fire only when 2–3 signals agree, against a personal baseline, with the reason shown to the leader.

HIGH

Network loss

Core monitoring stays on the bracelet; emergencies travel over local BLE Mesh without the cloud.

HIGH

Battery

Low-power design (~3 days), a 20% warning, wireless chargers in camps, and spare bracelets on hand.

MEDIUM

Bracelet removed

A skin-contact sensor and a secure fastener flag removal after five minutes.

HIGH

Pilgrim trust

Religious endorsement, visible daily value, and full silence during prayer earn the right to be worn.

Graceful degradation: even if the cloud, the tablet, and the mesh all fail, the simplest layer still protects the pilgrim — a red LED and strong vibration visible to anyone nearby.

Challenges

The hard parts· التحديات

01

Designing for a pilgrim who can't read the screen

Abu Salem can't read Arabic or English and has never worn a tracker. Every message had to land through colour and vibration alone — so the bracelet stays fully passive, with no text and nothing to learn.

02

Earning trust without crying wolf

One false alarm too many and the leader starts ignoring the bracelet. I combined signals, added a personal baseline, and surfaced the reason for each alert so the leader can judge it at a glance.

03

Sensitive data, real consent

Health and location are deeply personal. The design keeps processing on the device, uses pseudonymous IDs, and explains consent in the pilgrim's own language before anything is worn.

04

Working when the network doesn't

The hardest constraint: help has to keep flowing during a total cellular blackout. BLE Mesh relays and a fail-safe local alarm mean the system never goes fully dark.

Scenario walkthrough

Abu Salem's night, two ways

The same separation, the same failing vitals — and how the two outcomes diverge.

Without Aman

Found after ~90 minutes

He drifts off at a crowded crossing at 7:45 PM. The leader can't reach him; verbal calls are useless. By 8:30 his condition worsens, and he's eventually found unconscious — long after help was needed.

With Aman

Reunited safely by 9:00 PM

The bracelet flags separation as a warning, then danger as his vitals climb — and reaches the leader over mesh despite the blackout. He's guided to Abu Salem, who takes water and medication and rejoins the group, his ring back to green.
