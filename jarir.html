Jarirجرير

A ground-up information-architecture redesign of the Jarir Bookstore app — cutting the clutter so people find what they came for, faster.

📚
Overview

Rebuilding how Jarir is organized· نبذة

We surveyed and interviewed real shoppers and kept hearing the same thing — the app holds a lot, but it's hard to arrange and hard to find. So we rebuilt its information architecture from the ground up.

The premise

Jarir's catalog is huge — books, electronics, toys, art supplies, games. The problem was never the content; it was the structure. We redesigned six key pages, led by the home and search pages, and split the crowded electronics category into focused destinations.

Our approach was metaphor- and color-driven: every category gets a visual identity, so the eye can sort before the brain has to read.

The shift

From cluttered to clear

An audit of the live app surfaced the real pain: too much on every screen, repeated information, tiny fonts, and nested categories that quietly limited what you could reach.

The problem

Everything competes for attention

The home and search pages were dense with options, colors and ads. Information repeated, fonts were small, and choosing one category often hid the others you needed.

Jarir's redesign

One clear path to the product

A decluttered home, a calmer search, color-coded categories, and electronics split into four focused pages — so the journey to a product is short and obvious.

Objectives

What the redesign had to fix

Three goals shaped every screen we touched.

01

Declutter the core pages

Rework the home and search pages so the essentials breathe and ads stop drowning the content.

02

Restructure the categories

Apply a metaphor- and color-driven scheme so each category is instantly recognizable — and nothing limits another.

03

Make finding faster

Split the overloaded electronics category into phones, laptops, headphones and video games — and prove it's quicker.

Audit & research

What the audit and users revealed

A heuristic audit plus interviews with shoppers exposed three recurring problems.

Home

Too much, all at once

The homepage was crowded with options, ads and duplicate information — genuinely distracting before you even start.

Search

Categories that limit you

Picking a type inside search hid other valid types, and the small font made the categories hard to even read.

Users

They skip the structure

Interviewees searched by product name to dodge the category maze; on desktop, the icons were too small to be useful.
