تجربة كاملة بنيتها بيدي

A room-scale VR experience built in Unity through the official VR Development pathway — four missions that grow a bare XR rig into a furnished room you can grab objects in, teleport around, and control through a world-space menu with comfort settings of its own.

🎮
Overview

An empty room, made real in VR· نبذة

VR Room is a room-scale virtual-reality experience I built in Unity, following the engine's official VR Development pathway from a bare scene all the way to a furnished, interactive space you control entirely with your hands.

The premise

It begins as nothing but an XR rig — a head camera and two tracked controllers floating in an empty box. Mission by mission, that box grows into a place: a couch, a fireplace, a desk, a television and tall windows looking over a city. You can reach out and grab the objects on the table, teleport across the floor, turn to face any wall, and open a settings menu that lives inside the room itself.

I treated each mission as a real design brief, not a checklist. Where does the player land when the scene loads? How does someone who has never held a headset learn they can teleport? What happens when free movement makes them dizzy? The result is small but complete — rig, environment, interactions, locomotion, and comfort controls — a whole VR app I assembled from the ground up.

The build

Four missions, one growing room· المهام

The VR Development pathway grows the same scene in stages — each mission adds a new layer of capability on top of the last, from a no-code setup all the way to custom C# interactions.

Mission 01

Setup & locomotion

Opened the starter scene, learned what makes an XR rig different from a normal Unity camera, furnished the empty room, then added teleport areas and turning so I could move around and admire it from every angle.

Mission 02

Interactions & UI

Made the room respond: grabbable objects reached by a ray, plus a world-space welcome panel that greets the player with instructions and an OK button pressed with the controller.

Mission 03

Comfort & accessibility

Added a settings menu behind a gear icon with placeholder toggles — including snap turning — a close button back to the welcome panel, and a gentle fade-in so arriving in the world isn't jarring.

Mission 04

Custom interactions · C#

The programming mission — extending the XR Interaction Toolkit with custom interactable behaviour in C# across new environments, moving beyond the no-code building of the first three.

Core interactions

What your hands can do· التفاعلات

Six interactions carry the whole experience — each one designed so the player discovers it by trying, not by reading a manual mid-air.

Grab

Reach & pick up

A ray extends from each controller — aim at an object, pull the trigger, and it snaps into your hand to inspect or move around the room.

Teleport

Point to move

Holding the thumbstick casts a curved ray; release it over a valid teleport area and you're instantly there, re-oriented to face the right way.

Turning

Snap & continuous turn

One controller turns the view in fixed snaps, the other turns smoothly — so you can face any wall without ever standing up.

Toggle

Two rays, one button

Pressing the thumbstick or Button A flips each controller between the interaction ray and the teleport ray, with the change shown live to the player.

Interface

World-space menus

Floating canvases — the welcome panel and reset menu — sit inside the room and respond to the controller ray just like real buttons.

Comfort

Settings you control

A gear icon opens a panel of toggles, so motion comfort isn't fixed by the developer — the player decides what feels good.

Challenges

The hard parts· التحديات

01

Teaching VR with no manual

A first-time user can't read a tooltip floating mid-air. The welcome panel had to explain — in a few short lines — that the thumbstick toggles between grabbing and teleporting, before the player got lost in the room.

Onboarding in 3D
02

Motion that doesn't make you sick

Free movement looks impressive but turns stomachs. I leaned on teleport and snap turning as the defaults, then exposed comfort as a setting the player can change — rather than a choice locked in by me.

Comfort first
03

Making "grabbable" obvious

Nothing tells you an object can be picked up until you try. The reaching ray — and the way it changes colour when it lands on a valid target — does the explaining without a single word of instruction.

Visual affordance
04

Keeping the frames high

VR has to stay smooth or the illusion breaks. Watching the live stats overlay — triangles, draw calls, frame time — kept the room rich to look at while staying light enough to render comfortably.

Performance aware
